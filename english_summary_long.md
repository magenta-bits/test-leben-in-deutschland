# Detailed Summary: Life in Germany (Leben in Deutschland)

This document provides a comprehensive overview of all topics covered by the German "Leben in Deutschland" citizenship test. It is organized into ten thematic sections, each drawing on the 300 questions and correct answers from the test. The aim is to present the material as coherent, readable prose rather than isolated question-and-answer pairs, so that a reader can build a thorough understanding of the German state, its history, institutions, legal system, social structures, and cultural life.

---

## 1. The German State — Basic Facts and Constitutional Foundations

The Federal Republic of Germany — abbreviated FRG, or in German, "Bundesrepublik Deutschland" — is a country of approximately 83 million inhabitants in the heart of Europe. It is a **republic**, not a monarchy, and its national symbols reflect its democratic identity: the flag bears the colors black, red, and gold, while the heraldic animal of the state is the **eagle**, whose image is also displayed in the plenary hall of the German Bundestag as the Federal Eagle.

Germany is, at its core, a **democratic, social federal state**. The word "federal" means that the country is not governed from a single center but is instead divided into **16 federal states** (Bundesländer), each with its own government and substantial powers, particularly over education policy. Below the federal and state levels sit the **municipalities** — the lowest level of political administration — which manage local affairs such as public order through offices like the public order office (Ordnungsamt). Each federal state is headed by a **Minister-President**, with the exception of the city-states Berlin, Hamburg, and Bremen, whose leaders use different titles.

The foundation of Germany's legal and political order is its constitution, known as the **Basic Law** (Grundgesetz), which was established in 1949. Article 1 of the Basic Law enshrines the most fundamental value in the German constitutional order: the **inviolability of human dignity**. No law or government action may violate this principle. The Basic Law also guarantees a broad catalogue of **fundamental rights**, including freedom of opinion (the right to express one's views openly, including on the internet or against the government), freedom of faith and conscience, freedom of movement (the right to choose one's own place of residence), and freedom of the press. The right to **asylum** is a fundamental right in the Basic Law, but it applies specifically to foreigners — it is the only fundamental right in the catalogue that does not apply to German citizens.

Notably, the Basic Law does **not** guarantee equal wealth for all citizens — that is not part of the constitutional framework. What the Basic Law does explicitly prohibit is **forced labor**. Monetary fines, by contrast, are fully compatible with the Basic Law as a form of legal sanction.

The Basic Law is a living document that is meant to be protected. A party that actively fights against the constitution is considered **unconstitutional** and may be banned. Equally, even a majority in the Bundestag cannot abolish fundamental rights such as press freedom — these rights are protected against legislative interference. The court responsible for interpreting the Basic Law and resolving constitutional disputes is the **Federal Constitutional Court** (Bundesverfassungsgericht).

The German state is also defined as a **constitutional state** (Rechtsstaat), which means that both the government and all residents are equally bound by the laws. The state may not act arbitrarily — it must comply with the legal framework. Another core principle is **popular sovereignty**: all state power ultimately comes from the people, who exercise it primarily through elections.

When it comes to financing, the German state funds its many tasks — building roads, maintaining schools, ensuring social security — through **taxes and social insurance contributions**. It is explicitly not a task of the state to pay for citizens' vacation trips.

## 2. Government Institutions and the Three State Powers

Germany separates its state power into three branches, a system designed to prevent any single institution from accumulating unchecked authority. The three powers are the **legislature** (which makes laws), the **executive** (which governs and administers), and the **judiciary** (which applies the law through the courts). The press — though free and vital to democracy — is not a state power; it is independent.

The **Bundestag** is Germany's federal parliament and sits in Berlin. It is the body elected directly by the German people and is responsible for passing laws at the federal level. It is important to note that forming the Federal Cabinet is **not** a task of the Bundestag — that responsibility lies with the Federal Chancellor and President. The President of the Bundestag is customarily a member of the strongest parliamentary group. The **Federal Eagle** is displayed in the plenary chamber of the Bundestag as a symbol of the republic.

Alongside the Bundestag, the **Bundesrat** forms the second part of the legislature. The Bundesrat is composed of representatives from the governments of Germany's 16 federal states. It participates in federal legislation and must approve certain types of laws. Because the Bundesrat reflects state-level politics, a change in the governing majority in a federal state can shift the balance in the Bundesrat and thereby make federal governance more complicated for the ruling coalition.

The **Federal Chancellor** is the head of government. The Chancellor is elected by the Bundestag on a proposal from the Federal President, and leads the Federal Cabinet (Bundeskabinett). The Chancellor's office — the Federal Chancellery — is located in Berlin, and the Chancellor spends the majority of their working time there. The current Federal Chancellor is **Olaf Scholz**.

The **Federal President** is the head of state. Unlike the Chancellor, the Federal President is not elected directly by the citizens but is chosen by the **Federal Assembly** (Bundesversammlung), a body that convenes specifically for this purpose. The Federal President's primary role is representational — representing Germany domestically and internationally — but the President also fulfills key constitutional functions: proposing the Chancellor for election, and formally appointing federal ministers. The current Federal President is **Frank-Walter Steinmeier**.

Germany's main political parties include the **CDU** (Christian Democratic Union) and its Bavarian sister party the **CSU** (Christian Social Union), together forming the largest parliamentary group in the Bundestag; the **SPD** (Social Democratic Party of Germany); and the **FDP** (Free Democratic Party). A **"traffic light coalition"** is the colloquial name for a governing alliance of the SPD, FDP, and Alliance 90/The Greens (whose party colors correspond to the red, yellow, and green of a traffic light). Members of the same party within parliament form a **parliamentary group** (Fraktion). The **opposition** consists of all members of parliament who do not belong to the governing party or parties — their role is to scrutinize and **control the government**.

The **Bundeswehr** is Germany's armed forces, its national army. It is a constitutional institution under civilian oversight.

## 3. Elections and Democratic Participation

Democratic self-governance in Germany is exercised primarily through regular, free elections. The **Bundestag** is elected every **four years** by all German citizens who are at least **18 years old**. Eligible voters receive an **election notification from their municipality** before each election, and it is permitted to cast one's vote **by mail** if one cannot attend a polling station in person.

The principles governing elections in Germany are that they must be **free** (no voter may be coerced or face disadvantages for how they vote), **equal** (every vote carries the same weight), and **secret** (the voting process is anonymous). These three principles — freedom, equality, and secrecy — are the cornerstones of legitimate democratic elections.

Germany uses a **combined majority and proportional representation system**. Each voter casts two votes: a first vote for a direct candidate in their constituency, and a second vote for a party. The **second votes** determine how many seats each party receives in the Bundestag in proportion to the overall vote share. The more second votes a party receives, the more seats it holds in parliament.

To prevent excessive fragmentation of the Bundestag, German electoral law contains a **5% threshold** (Fünf-Prozent-Hürde): a party must receive at least 5% of the second votes nationwide to be represented in the Bundestag at all. This rule exists because having many very small parties in parliament makes coalition-building and stable government formation significantly more difficult.

The right to **vote** is technically called **active suffrage**; the right to stand for election is called passive suffrage. Participating in democratic elections is a **right** — it is not compulsory in Germany, unlike in some other countries. Elections also serve the important democratic function of enabling a **change of government** in accordance with the majority will of voters.

**Election helpers** are volunteers who assist on election day, most importantly by counting the votes after polls close. Members of parliament who are elected by the citizens are called **representatives** (Abgeordnete). If members of parliament switch parliamentary groups, the governing coalition can lose its majority — illustrating how the composition of the Bundestag directly affects political stability.

Citizens can also participate in democracy beyond elections. They may organize or join a **citizens' initiative** to advocate for local issues — for example, to prevent the cancellation of a bus route. Public **demonstrations** are also a right, but they must be **registered with the authorities** in advance. At the local level, a **mayor** is the elected head of a municipality, and the **public order office** is the local authority responsible for enforcing regulations. There are also approximately **40 Jewish Makkabi sports clubs** in Germany, which are open to all people — an example of inclusive civil society institutions.

## 4. German History — National Socialism and World War II

Germany's history in the twentieth century is defined by a catastrophic period of dictatorship and war that left lasting consequences for the country and the world. Understanding this history is essential to understanding modern Germany.

In **1933**, Adolf Hitler was appointed **Reich Chancellor** of Germany. Within a short time, the National Socialists (Nazis) had dismantled the Weimar Republic's democratic institutions and established a **dictatorship** — the so-called **Third Reich** — that lasted until 1945. Under National Socialism, free elections were abolished, political parties were banned, and forced labor was introduced. The NS state was characterized above all by a policy of **state racism**, which placed certain groups — particularly Jewish people, Sinti and Roma, people with disabilities, and others — outside the protection of the law and made them targets of persecution, forced labor, and ultimately murder.

On **November 9, 1938**, a nationwide pogrom known as **Kristallnacht** (Night of Broken Glass) took place: National Socialists and their supporters destroyed Jewish shops, businesses, and synagogues across Germany. This event marked a dramatic escalation in the regime's persecution of Jewish people.

Not all Germans accepted the NS regime passively. The most famous act of resistance came on **July 20, 1944**, when **Claus Schenk Graf von Stauffenberg** and co-conspirators carried out a nearly successful assassination attempt against Hitler. The plot failed, and Stauffenberg and his fellow conspirators were executed.

**World War II** lasted from **1939 to 1945**. It ended in Europe on **May 8, 1945**, with Germany's **unconditional surrender** to the Allied powers. After the war, Germany was divided into **four occupation zones** administered by the four Allied Occupation Powers: the **USA**, the **Soviet Union**, **Great Britain**, and **France** (not Japan, which was a defeated Axis power, not an occupier of Germany). The capital, **Berlin**, was likewise divided into **four sectors**. From June 1948 to May 1949, the Soviet Union blocked all land access routes to West Berlin, forcing the Western Allies to supply the city by airlift — one of the most dramatic episodes of the early Cold War.

Throughout Germany today, small brass plaques called **Stolpersteine** (stumbling stones) are embedded in pavements outside former homes and workplaces, commemorating the victims of National Socialism. Every year on **January 27** — the date of the liberation of the Auschwitz extermination camp in 1945 — Germany observes an official day of remembrance for the victims of National Socialism. **Denying the Holocaust** is not only anti-Semitic — it is a **criminal offense** in Germany, punishable by up to **five years of imprisonment or a fine**. Similarly, publicly calling for the **destruction of the State of Israel** is prohibited under German law. In 1970, Chancellor **Willy Brandt** famously knelt before the monument at the former Jewish ghetto in Warsaw as a gesture of humility and a request for forgiveness from Poland and the Polish Jewish community — one of the most powerful acts of political atonement in postwar history. Germany's **special responsibility toward Israel** is rooted directly in the crimes committed against Jews during the NS period.

## 5. German History — Postwar Division and Reunification

In the immediate aftermath of World War II, Germany's political future was deeply uncertain. Out of the postwar occupation zones, two separate German states emerged in **1949**. The **Federal Republic of Germany** (FRG) was established in the western zones, adopting a democratic, market-based constitutional order. In the same year, the **German Democratic Republic** (GDR) was founded in the Soviet occupation zone in the east. Despite having "democratic" in its name, the GDR was in practice a **dictatorship** — a one-party state dominated by the Socialist Unity Party (SED).

The FRG's first Federal Chancellor was **Konrad Adenauer**, who steered West Germany through its formative years and anchored it firmly in the Western alliance. The FRG experienced an extraordinary period of economic growth in the **1950s**, commonly known as the **"economic miracle"** (Wirtschaftswunder), which rapidly raised living standards in the western state.

The GDR, by contrast, operated a **planned economy** in which the state controlled the allocation of resources rather than leaving it to market forces. The GDR was a member of the **Warsaw Pact**, the Soviet-led military alliance of Eastern Bloc countries, and was politically and economically integrated into the Soviet sphere. The "**Iron Curtain**" was the term used to describe the ideological, political, and physical barrier separating Warsaw Pact countries from the Western democracies. The GDR's distinctive flag featured the black-red-gold of the German tricolor overlaid with a **hammer, compass, and wreath of grain**.

The GDR's repressive nature was maintained partly through its feared **Stasi** — short for **Staatssicherheit**, the Ministry for State Security — a vast secret police apparatus that monitored, infiltrated, and suppressed dissent among the population. On **June 17, 1953**, workers in the GDR launched **nationwide strikes and a popular uprising** against poor working conditions and political repression. The uprising was suppressed, but it was commemorated for decades in West Germany as a public holiday.

The most visible symbol of Germany's division was the **Berlin Wall**, built by the GDR in **1961** to stem the mass emigration of its citizens to the West. From 1961 until 1989, Berlin was physically **divided by the Wall**. The Wall fell on **November 9, 1989**, after a wave of pro-democracy mass protests known as the **"Monday demonstrations"** (Montagsdemonstrationen), in which crowds famously chanted "**We are the people**" (Wir sind das Volk). This period is called the **"Turning Point"** (Die Wende) because the GDR transformed politically from a dictatorship into a democracy during this time.

German **reunification** followed rapidly. On **October 3, 1990** — now a national holiday — the **five eastern federal states** (Brandenburg, Saxony, Saxony-Anhalt, Thuringia, and Mecklenburg-Western Pomerania) joined the Federal Republic of Germany. This act of accession brought those states into the FRG's legal and constitutional framework and simultaneously made them members of the **European Union**. Germany has had its **current borders** since 1990. The GDR had existed as a separate state from **1949 to 1990**. Federal Chancellor **Helmut Kohl** is known as the "**Chancellor of German Unity**" for his decisive role in driving the reunification process forward.

In terms of foreign policy, it was **Willy Brandt** — as Foreign Minister and later Chancellor — who pioneered the **Ostpolitik** (Eastern policy), seeking reconciliation and normalized relations between West Germany and the countries of Eastern Europe, a policy that laid important groundwork for the eventual end of the Cold War.

## 6. Law, Courts, and Rights

Germany's legal system is built on the principle of the **constitutional state** (Rechtsstaat): the state must act in accordance with the law, and all persons are equal before it. Laws prohibit the **unequal treatment of citizens by the state**, meaning that no one may be discriminated against on the basis of, for example, disability, skin color, or other personal characteristics. The **equal treatment principle** is a cornerstone of the legal order: a person may not be denied a job simply because they are in a wheelchair, have dark skin, or are a mother.

Within the constitutional state, the three state powers are kept separate. **Judges** belong to the **judiciary** — they work at courts independently and are responsible for **administering justice** by pronouncing judgments. A judge's independence is fundamental: they must not be subject to political pressure from the legislature or executive. A court **trial** takes place in the context of **criminal proceedings**, when a person is accused of breaking the law.

**Lay judges** (Schöffen) are an important feature of the German court system. These are ordinary citizens who serve as voluntary judges alongside professional judges, participating in determining both **guilt and punishment**. German citizens can be required by law to take on the **honorary office of juror**, making this one of the civic duties that may be assigned to citizens. A **lawyer** (Rechtsanwalt or Rechtsanwältin) advises individuals on legal questions and represents them before courts.

For disputes in the workplace, the relevant court is the **Labor Court** (Arbeitsgericht). If an employee has a conflict with their employer — for example, if the employer fails to pay wages — the employee can **file a lawsuit at the Labor Court**. The **police** play an important role in the rule-of-law state as well: their primary task is to **monitor compliance with laws** and maintain public order.

Freedom of opinion, while a fundamental right, is not unlimited. It is restricted specifically when a person **publicly spreads false factual claims about another individual** — this crosses the line from protected opinion into unlawful defamation. The Holocaust denial is treated with particular seriousness: it is both an **example of anti-Semitic behavior** and a criminal offense punishable by **up to five years of imprisonment or a fine**. Publicly calling for the destruction of Israel is similarly prohibited.

Democracy itself is characterized by certain principles — free elections, multiple parties, the rule of law — and equally defined by what it **excludes**: press censorship, arrests by private police without legal basis, and the absence of legal oversight are all incompatible with a democratic constitutional state.

When citizens encounter incorrect decisions by authorities — such as an incorrect tax assessment or an incorrect bill from a government authority — they have the right to **file an objection** (Widerspruch). If a citizen feels mistreated by a civil servant, they can **complain to the head of the authority**.

## 7. Social System, Economy, and Working Life

Germany operates what is known as a **social market economy** (soziale Marktwirtschaft). This economic model is based on **supply and demand** — prices and production are primarily determined by the market — but it is paired with a strong commitment by the state to ensure **social balance**. The state intervenes to protect citizens from the worst effects of economic hardship and to provide essential public infrastructure and services.

Most working people in Germany are **employed by a company or government agency**. All permanently employed workers automatically pay into the **social insurance system**, with contributions shared equally between employers and employees. The statutory social insurance system in Germany comprises four branches: **health insurance** (Krankenversicherung), **long-term care insurance** (Pflegeversicherung), **pension insurance** (Rentenversicherung), and **unemployment insurance** (Arbeitslosenversicherung). **Life insurance** is a private financial product and is explicitly **not** part of statutory social insurance. Similarly, **value-added tax (VAT)** is a consumer tax on goods and services — it is not deducted from an employee's salary.

The state uses tax revenues and social contributions to finance its tasks: building roads and schools, funding healthcare and social welfare, and maintaining the legal and administrative system. What the state does **not** do is pay for citizens' private vacation trips — that falls outside the scope of state responsibility.

**Trade unions** (Gewerkschaften) are organizations that represent the collective interests of **employees** in negotiations with employers over wages, working conditions, and rights. Within individual companies, employees are represented by a **works council** (Betriebsrat), which helps workers address problems with their employer and ensures that employee rights are respected in the workplace.

Legal protections in the workplace are robust. A pregnant woman **cannot be dismissed** because of her pregnancy — this is protected by **maternity protection** law. When ending employment voluntarily, an employee must observe the agreed **notice period**. If a job applicant is rejected based on discriminatory grounds — such as being pregnant or having a disability — this constitutes **discrimination** and is prohibited.

For those seeking training and career guidance, the **Career Information Center (BIZ)** at the Federal Employment Agency helps young people and adults with **apprenticeship searches** and career planning. The world of work is also changing rapidly, and **lifelong learning** — the willingness to continuously update one's skills and knowledge — is considered particularly important for professional success today and in the future.

For outstanding contributions to society — in political, economic, cultural, intellectual, or social fields — individuals can be honored with the **Order of Merit of the Federal Republic of Germany** (Bundesverdienstkreuz), the country's highest civilian decoration.

## 8. The European Union, International Relations, and Germany's Neighbors

Germany is not an isolated nation but one deeply embedded in the European and international community. It is a **founding member of the European Union**, having been among the states that signed the **Treaties of Rome in 1957**, which established the **European Economic Community (EEC)** — the forerunner of today's EU. When Germany was reunified in 1990, the five eastern federal states that joined the FRG simultaneously became part of the European Union.

Today, the EU has **27 member states**. The **European Parliament** is the EU's directly elected legislative body; it is elected every **five years** and sits in **Strasbourg**. European integration — the process of bringing European states closer together economically, politically, and legally — was driven in part by the shared goal of ensuring **peace and security** across the continent after two devastating world wars. This spirit of reconciliation was powerfully symbolized when French President François Mitterrand and German Chancellor Helmut Kohl stood together at the battlefield of **Verdun** to commemorate the dead of both world wars.

Germany is also a member of the **Schengen Area**, a zone within which border controls between participating countries have been removed, allowing people to travel freely across many European countries **without passport control**. Germany introduced the **euro** as its currency, and euro cash has been in circulation in Germany since **2002**.

Germany shares land borders with **nine neighboring countries**: to the north, **Denmark** and the **Netherlands**; to the west, the **Netherlands**, **Belgium**, **Luxembourg**, and **France**; to the south, **Switzerland** and **Austria**; and to the east, the **Czech Republic** and **Poland**. Germany is also notable for the fact that a large **German-speaking population** lives in neighboring **Austria**, reflecting the shared linguistic heritage of the two countries.

Regarding the **State of Israel**, its founding was based on a **United Nations resolution**. Germany bears a **special responsibility** toward Israel — rooted in the crimes committed against Jewish people during the National Socialist period — and German law therefore expressly prohibits publicly calling for the **destruction of Israel**.

## 9. Daily Life, Residence, and Legal Matters

Germany has a well-developed framework of laws and institutions governing everyday life, from childhood education to housing, marriage, and consumer rights.

**Education** is compulsory for children in Germany: most children are subject to **compulsory schooling** and **compulsory school attendance**. Parents or guardians decide whether their child attends kindergarten, but children aged **three to school age** have a legal **right to a kindergarten place**. For higher education, the **Abitur** — the school-leaving certificate awarded after completing the highest level of secondary school — is the standard requirement for university admission. Adults who wish to obtain the Abitur later in life can do so at an **evening gymnasium** (Abendgymnasium). The **adult education center** (Volkshochschule) is a widely available institution for **continuing education** across a broad range of subjects.

The **age of majority** in Germany is **18**: from this point, a person is legally an adult and responsible for their own decisions. A young adult of 22, for example, has the full right to live with a partner of her choosing, and her parents must accept her decision even if they disapprove. **Criminal responsibility** begins at the age of **14**: young people aged 14 and over who commit criminal offenses are subject to legal punishment, though under the youth criminal law framework rather than the adult penal code.

**Parents** are primarily responsible for raising their children in Germany. However, the state also has a protective role: the **youth welfare office** (Jugendamt) supports families experiencing difficulties and, if a child is being physically abused or neglected, has the authority to **remove the child from the family**. Hitting a child is **punishable by law** in Germany.

**Maternity protection** (Mutterschutz) provides legal protection for pregnant women before and after childbirth, including job security. A woman cannot be dismissed because she is pregnant. **Parental allowance** (Elterngeld) is a financial benefit for parents in the period after the birth of a child, but it must be **applied for** at the parental allowance office — it is not paid automatically.

For marriage, a couple must first go to the **registry office** (Standesamt) for a civil ceremony. **Polygamy** — being married to more than one person at the same time — is **not permitted** in Germany. In the event of a **divorce**, the process requires the involvement of a **lawyer**, and a judge will typically divide marital assets. Unmarried couples who are of legal age may live together freely; the law does not require parental consent. However, a 13-year-old and a 25-year-old may **not** live together as a couple — this is prohibited under child protection law.

Moving to a new address within Germany requires registration at the **residents' registration office** (Einwohnermeldeamt). After 10 PM, **statutory night rest** begins in residential buildings, and residents are expected to keep noise to a minimum. Most apartment buildings also have **house rules** (Hausordnung) that set out the specific regulations all residents must follow.

For activities in public life: holding a **demonstration** requires prior registration with the authorities. Obtaining a driver's license is a prerequisite before buying a car — the license must come first. Opening a restaurant requires a **restaurant license** from the competent authority.

German law also protects the **secrecy of correspondence**: intentionally opening a letter addressed to someone else is a legal violation. The **principle of equal treatment** prohibits discriminating against people on grounds such as disability, skin color, or family status — a woman with children may not be refused a job on that basis alone.

On financial matters: permanently employed workers do **not** pay **value-added tax** from their salary — VAT is a consumer tax, not an income deduction. If a citizen receives an incorrect **tax assessment** or an incorrect bill from a public authority, they have the right to file a **formal objection** (Widerspruch). If a product purchased in Germany turns out to be defective, the buyer has the right to **return it for complaint** (Reklamation). A **13-year-old** is permitted to read a political newspaper freely — no special permission or parental action is required.

## 10. Religion, Culture, and Migration

Germany is a country of diverse religious traditions, long-established cultural customs, and a history shaped by migration. Understanding these dimensions is important both for integration and for participation in civic life.

The relationship between **church and state** in Germany is one of separation: there is no state church, and the government does not direct religious affairs. At the same time, Germany does maintain a **church tax** (Kirchensteuer) system, whereby members of recognized religious communities pay a supplementary tax linked to their income and wage tax. Whether a person belongs to a church must be declared on their **tax return**; those who formally leave their church are no longer subject to the church tax.

**Christianity** is the religion that has most profoundly shaped **German and European culture** — in art, architecture, philosophy, law, and calendar customs alike. Christian traditions are embedded in the rhythm of public life, though Germany practices **religious tolerance**: every person is free to believe what they wish, and people of different faiths can share public spaces together. For example, a Christian and a Muslim can both use a public swimming pool — religion does not restrict access to public facilities.

**Jewish life** in Germany is ancient and historically significant. Jewish communities have existed in the territory of present-day Germany for approximately **1,700 years**, making them one of the oldest continuous presences in German history. Today, the largest Jewish communities are in **Berlin** and **Munich**. The Jewish house of worship is called a **synagogue**. There are approximately **40 Jewish Makkabi sports clubs** across Germany, which are open to people of all backgrounds. The Holocaust denial is treated as both a form of anti-Semitism and a criminal offense — a recognition that protecting Jewish life and memory is a permanent responsibility of the German state and society.

Several Christian holidays and traditions are integral to German life. **Pentecost** (Pfingsten) is a Christian holiday. The **Advent season** — the four weeks before Christmas — is a period of anticipation and preparation. At **Christmas**, a beloved custom is **decorating a Christmas tree**. **Easter** is marked by the custom of **painting eggs**. During the **Carnival** season (Fasching/Karneval), people wear colorful costumes and masks, and the highlight is **Rose Monday** (Rosenmontag), when elaborate parades take place in many German cities.

Germany has also been profoundly shaped by **migration**. In the 1950s and 1960s, West Germany signed agreements to recruit workers from abroad to meet labor shortages during the economic miracle. These workers, known as **guest workers** (Gastarbeiter), first came from **Italy** and later from a range of other countries. Over time, the largest group of migrants to settle permanently in Germany came from **Turkey**, making the Turkish community the largest migrant community in the country. Many Germans today have family roots in these migration histories.

Volunteering is also a significant feature of German civil society. A large number of Germans work **voluntarily and without pay** in clubs, associations, and charitable organizations during their free time — contributing to sports, culture, welfare, and many other areas of public life.

Finally, the **German national anthem** begins with the words "**Unity and justice and freedom**" (Einigkeit und Recht und Freiheit) — a phrase that captures the values at the heart of the Federal Republic and the aspirations that have guided it since 1949.
