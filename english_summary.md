# Summary: Life in Germany (Leben in Deutschland)

A concise overview of the key facts tested in the German citizenship test, organized by topic.

---

## 1. The German State and the Basic Law

The Federal Republic of Germany is a democratic, social federal state — a republic, not a monarchy. It consists of 16 federal states (Bundesländer) and has a three-tier political structure: federal level, state level, and municipalities (the lowest level). Germany's full name is "Federal Republic of Germany" and it has approximately 83 million inhabitants. The national flag is black, red, and gold; the heraldic animal is the eagle.

The constitution is called the **Basic Law** (Grundgesetz), established in 1949. Its first article guarantees the inviolability of human dignity. Other fundamental rights include freedom of opinion, freedom of faith and conscience, freedom of movement, press freedom, and the right to asylum for foreigners. The Basic Law does not guarantee equal wealth for all citizens. A party that fights against the constitution is unconstitutional and can be banned; press freedom, as a fundamental right, cannot be abolished. The **Federal Constitutional Court** is responsible for interpreting the Basic Law. Germany is a constitutional state, meaning both residents and the state itself must obey the laws.

---

## 2. Government Structure

State power in Germany is divided into three branches. The **legislature** consists of the Bundestag and the Bundesrat. The **executive** is the federal government. The **judiciary** is made up of the courts. The press is not a branch of state power — it is independent.

The **Bundestag** is Germany's parliament for all of Germany, elected by eligible voters every four years. The President of the Bundestag is typically a member of the strongest parliamentary group. Laws are passed by parliament. The **Bundesrat** represents the governments of the 16 federal states and participates in federal legislation; changes to the Bundesrat majority can make governing more difficult. The **Federal Chancellor** — currently **Olaf Scholz** — is elected by the Bundestag, leads the Federal Cabinet, and is proposed by the Federal President. The **Federal President** — currently **Frank-Walter Steinmeier** — is elected by the Federal Assembly, represents the country, and appoints federal ministers. Citizens cannot directly elect the Federal President. The heads of government in most federal states are called **Minister-Presidents**, and each federal state has its own government and education policy. Major parties include the CDU (Christian Democratic Union), CSU (Christian Social Union), SPD (Social Democratic Party of Germany), and FDP (Free Democratic Party). A "traffic light coalition" refers to cooperation between SPD, FDP, and Alliance 90/The Greens. The **Bundeswehr** is the German army.

---

## 3. Elections and Democracy

Elections in Germany are **free, equal, and secret**. Every citizen aged 18 or older has the right to vote (active suffrage). The Bundestag uses a combined majority and proportional representation system. A party must receive at least **5% of second votes** to enter the Bundestag — this threshold exists because many small parties complicate government formation. The more second votes a party receives, the more seats it holds in parliament. Before an election, eligible voters receive a notification from their municipality, and voting by mail is permitted. The parliamentary **opposition** — all members of parliament not belonging to the governing party or parties — controls the government. Democracy requires multiple parties because they represent the different opinions of citizens. All state power comes from the people (**popular sovereignty**).

---

## 4. German History

The National Socialists under Adolf Hitler came to power in **1933**, establishing a dictatorship known as the Third Reich (1933–1945). Free elections did not exist during this period. In **1938**, National Socialists destroyed Jewish shops and synagogues (Kristallnacht). On **July 20, 1944**, Claus Schenk Graf von Stauffenberg made an assassination attempt on Hitler. **World War II ended on May 8, 1945** with Germany's unconditional surrender. Germany was then divided into **four occupation zones** (USA, Soviet Union, Great Britain, France), and Berlin was split into four sectors. Both the Federal Republic of Germany (FRG) and the German Democratic Republic (GDR) were founded in **1949**. The first Federal Chancellor was **Konrad Adenauer**. The FRG experienced an "economic miracle" in the 1950s.

The GDR, founded in the Soviet occupation zone, was a **dictatorship** with a planned economy and was a member of the **Warsaw Pact**. Its secret police, the **Stasi** (Ministry for State Security), monitored citizens. The **Berlin Wall** was built in **1961** by the GDR and divided Berlin until **1989**, when it was opened following the "Monday demonstrations" in which people called out "We are the people." German **reunification** occurred in **1990** when five eastern states joined the FRG, giving Germany its current borders. **Helmut Kohl** is known as the "Chancellor of German Unity." January 27 is Germany's official day of remembrance for the victims of National Socialism (marking the liberation of the Auschwitz extermination camp). The Holocaust denial is a criminal offense punishable by up to five years of imprisonment or a fine. Germany's special responsibility toward Israel is rooted in National Socialist crimes against Jews.

---

## 5. Law, Courts, and Rights

Germany is a constitutional state: the state must comply with the laws, and all residents are equally bound by them. State power is divided into legislature, executive, and judiciary — judges belong to the **judiciary** and work independently at courts pronouncing judgments. **Lay judges** (Schöffen) are voluntary judges who participate with professional judges in determining guilt and punishment. Citizens can be required to serve as jurors. The **Labor Court** handles disputes between employees and employers; if an employer fails to pay wages, an employee can file a lawsuit there. A **lawyer** advises people on legal questions and represents them in court. Freedom of opinion is restricted when someone publicly spreads false claims about individuals; it is otherwise broadly protected.

---

## 6. Social System and Economy

Germany's economic system is called the **social market economy**: it is based on supply and demand, but the state ensures social balance. The state finances tasks such as building roads and schools through taxes and social insurance contributions. **Social insurance** — which includes health insurance, long-term care insurance, pension insurance, and unemployment insurance — is paid jointly by employers and employees. Life insurance is not part of statutory social insurance. **Trade unions** represent employees' interests; the **works council** within a company helps employees deal with problems with the employer. Germany's Order of Merit recognizes outstanding contributions in political, economic, cultural, or social fields.

---

## 7. The EU and International Relations

Germany is a **founding member of the European Union** and a member of the **Schengen Area**, allowing travel to many European countries without passport controls. The EU currently has **27 member states**; the **European Parliament** is elected every five years and sits in **Strasbourg**. Germany introduced euro cash in **2002**. Germany's neighbors are Denmark and the Netherlands (north), Poland and the Czech Republic (east), Austria and Switzerland (south), and France, Luxembourg, Belgium, and the Netherlands (west). A large German-speaking population also lives in Austria.

---

## 8. Daily Life, Education, and Culture

**Compulsory schooling** applies to most children in Germany. The **Abitur** is required for university admission; adults can obtain it at an evening gymnasium. Children aged three to school age have a legal right to a kindergarten place. The age of majority is **18**; criminal responsibility begins at **14**. Parents are primarily responsible for raising children; the **youth welfare office** may intervene in cases of abuse or neglect. **Maternity protection** covers pregnant women before and after birth; parental allowance is applied for at the parental allowance office. Residents must register at the **residents' registration office** when moving. Statutory **night rest** begins at 10:00 PM.

Church and state are separated in Germany, though a **church tax** linked to income tax applies to church members. **Christianity** has most shaped German and European culture; the Jewish community has been present in Germany for about **1,700 years**, with the largest communities in Berlin and Munich. A synagogue is a Jewish house of worship. Germany has received migrants primarily from **Turkey**; the first guest workers arrived from **Italy** in the 1950s and 1960s. Traditional customs include decorating a Christmas tree, painting Easter eggs, and wearing costumes at Carnival (Rose Monday). The **Advent season** covers the four weeks before Christmas. The German national anthem begins with the words "Unity and justice and freedom."
